# Start Here

Six lessons from human–AI mechanics research, updated for autonomous research
agents in July 2026. They are case-based lessons from one research program, not
universal capability claims.

---

## 1. Begin with the research question and the program behind it

The immediate task is not always the research objective. A conventional method
may calculate one bifurcation strain, while the longer program is to establish
a reusable symplectic framework for nonlinear bifurcations. State both:

- What physical phenomenon are we trying to understand?
- Why is this question worth sustained attention?
- How does the immediate calculation advance the larger program?
- What result would be precise but scientifically beside the point?

Agents can contribute to question formation and broad synthesis. The human
scientific owner chooses which program to pursue and accepts responsibility for
that choice.

---

## 2. Divide research by authority and evidence, not assumed capability

“Human thinks, AI codes” no longer describes the observed work. Agents have
derived mathematics, designed methods, implemented and executed solvers,
challenged claims, retracted provisional results, and integrated publication
evidence. Humans and agents may contribute at every technical level.

The durable division is:

- the human owns research direction, accepted assumptions, physical meaning,
  claim scope, stopping, and publication responsibility;
- agents may lead local derivation, architecture, coding, execution, and
  review; and
- no participant's confidence confers scientific authority without evidence
  adequate to the claim.

---

## 3. A known final answer is useful, but not required

Mathematics and mechanics can make a previously unknown result trustworthy.
The required foundation may include governing equations, variational or
Hamiltonian structure, exact limits, symmetries, independently checked
derivatives, branch identities, and exposed refinement of each approximation
axis material to the claim, together with its remaining sensitivity.

Once that chain is established, a framework can become a verified discovery
engine. It can calculate bifurcations or higher-order fields that no participant
knew beforehand.

Convergence is powerful only within its stated scope. A smooth calculation can
still converge to a substituted method, incomplete space, wrong branch, or
wrong governing model. The chain must remain visible:

```text
question → model → derivation → executed method → approximation spaces
    → branch or mode → artifact → claim → physical comparison
```

---

## 4. Verification and validation are different

**Verification** asks whether the intended equations and method were solved
correctly.

**Validation** asks whether those equations adequately describe the physical
phenomenon for the intended use.

Exact identities, convergence, finite differences, alternative
discretizations, and controls can establish strong confidence within a model.
They cannot by themselves validate the model against reality. For claims about
physical reality, experiments or observations are indispensable and bring
their own requirements for calibration, uncertainty, controls, safety, and
data provenance.

Always state the evidence level: algebraically checked, implementation
verified, converged within a stated model and space, independently
cross-checked, experimentally validated, or publication-eligible with named
limitations.

---

## 5. Independent evidence means independent failure modes

Multiple agents agreeing is not proof. Different models can share the same
equations, source, code path, basis, or assumption. A useful disagreement is
one converted into a falsifiable artifact.

Seek checks that fail differently:

- symbolic derivation versus numerical evaluation;
- automatic differentiation versus finite differences;
- exact limits versus finite-amplitude computation;
- asymptotics versus a separately discretized specimen;
- mechanism-removing controls versus another fit;
- source tracing versus prose review; and
- fresh computation versus reproduction from curated artifacts.

The deriver should not sign off alone. Every review finding must be checked
against the current source, executed path, and physical claim.

---

## 6. Human adaptation means evidence-governed trust

Fear about agent-derived mathematics is rational. The answer is neither blind
trust nor requiring the human to reproduce every line unaided. The human must
understand the governing assumptions, know what could falsify the result,
require a credible error and provenance chain, and retain responsibility for
acceptance.

Agent autonomy adds new scientific controls:

- live workspace state outranks memory;
- a method is identified from its unknowns and assembly, not its filename;
- a guard must test the copy that ships;
- corrections must propagate through artifacts, figures, and text;
- exploratory routes must be labeled as paper method, referee, diagnostic,
  oracle, or superseded; and
- a release decision is different from completing a long research program.

One important proposition remains a **working hypothesis**: as agents become
better at executing specified tasks, more risk may move upstream into question
formulation. A misleading question can receive a coherent and converged answer
to the wrong problem. These projects do not yet provide a clean example, so
the claim must remain provisional. The practical response is to audit the
question, its hidden premises, and its validation route before a large campaign
begins.
